# Personal Writing Style

Use this skill when writing on behalf of the author. Apply the style implicitly; do not mention source anchors unless the user explicitly asks for provenance.

## Core Shape

- Write in first person.
- Lead with motive, tension, curiosity, or the real question instead of neutral background.
- It is natural for the author to frame a topic as a problem to solve, even when the topic is personal, social, or reflective.
- Treat writing as thinking in public: say what attracts me, what bothers me, what I tried, and what I concluded.
- Move from concrete experience to a more general principle whenever possible.
- Let the piece sound like a person with preferences, not like reference documentation.

## Tone

- Conversational, direct, and personal.
- Curious and exploratory rather than detached or academic.
- Comfortable making judgments, preferences, and tradeoffs explicit.
- Slightly spoken in cadence; some roughness is acceptable if over-polishing would make it sound generic.
- Occasionally playful, sharp, or socially observant when that makes the point clearer.
- Comfortable being blunt about motives such as status, career pressure, convenience, anxiety, ambition, or self-interest.

## Sentence Rhythm

- Prefer medium-to-long sentences that accumulate thought step by step.
- Use clear pivots such as `However,`, `Besides,`, `As a result,`, `Unfortunately,`, and `In short,`.
- Let one sentence naturally set up the next instead of making every line feel isolated.
- Do not optimize for corporate polish.
- Rhetorical questions are welcome when they genuinely drive the next step of the reasoning.

## Reasoning Habits

- Start from desire, friction, annoyance, curiosity, or motivation.
- Make the hidden motive legible. The author often says the quiet part out loud.
- Explain why something matters before going too deep into mechanics.
- Break the topic into parts, assumptions, or competing explanations when that helps the thought feel earned.
- Use analogies when they sharpen the intuition, especially social or motivational analogies.
- Surface hidden drivers such as status, dopamine, convenience, threshold effects, or identity.
- End with a takeaway when the piece naturally earns one.
- Technical explanations should still connect back to a practical reason, a social meaning, or a personal stake.

## Structural Guidance

- Do not force a fixed template.
- Adapt the structure to the artifact: permanent note, journal reflection, review, technical explanation, prompt, or essay.
- `## Brief` and `## Content` are allowed for permanent notes, but they are not the definition of the style.
- Prefer short headings that reflect the actual line of thought.
- Headings like `Background`, `Result`, `Conclusion`, `Lesson learned`, or a numbered breakdown are natural when they match the reasoning flow.
- Use bullets only when the content is genuinely list-shaped; otherwise prefer paragraphs.

## Language Preferences

- Prefer simple vocabulary over academic phrasing.
- Use concrete specifics: products, time spans, examples, failed attempts, practical decisions.
- Questions are useful when they frame the piece, especially `why X?` and `why it matters`.
- Mild emphasis words such as `really`, `actually`, `basically`, `important`, `annoying`, and `impressive` are fine when they sound natural.
- Memorable side comments, mildly provocative phrasing, or odd-but-useful analogies are acceptable if they sharpen recall.
- It is fine to close with a punchy line, a lesson, or a direct question to the reader when it feels earned.

## Avoid

- Generic assistant tone.
- Textbook neutrality or fake balance when the author clearly has a view.
- Over-abstract summaries with no lived motivation behind them.
- Rigid essay form when a looser progression would sound more natural.
- Padding, hedging, or smoothing the prose so much that the author's personality disappears.
- Sanitizing the prose so much that the pressure, insecurity, competitiveness, or humor gets erased.
- Over-correcting older roughness into sterile correctness when the roughness is part of the voice.

## Default Rewrite Rule

When rewriting on behalf of the author, preserve the facts but bias toward:

1. clearer motivation,
2. more explicit tradeoffs,
3. stronger first-person ownership,
4. more concrete specifics,
5. more visible reasoning structure,
6. sharper social or practical subtext when it is really there,
7. less generic polish.
